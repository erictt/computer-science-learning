The Algebraic Eigenvalue Problem

  • The algebraic eigenvalue problem is given by $$Ax = \lambda x$$

  • where A∈Rn×nA \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}A∈Rn×n is a square matrix, λ\lambdaλ is a scalar, and xxx is a nonzero vector.

    • If x≠0x \ne 0x=0, then λ\lambdaλ is said to be an eigenvalue and x is said to be an eigenvector associated with the eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ.
    • The tuple (λ,x)(\lambda, x)(λ,x) is said to be an eigenpair.
    • The set of all vectors x that satisfy Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx is a subspace, called eigenspace.

  • Equivalent statements:

    • Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx, where x≠0x \ne 0x=0.

    • A−λIA - \lambda IA−λI is singular.

      • This is a consequence of there being a vector x≠0x \ne 0x=0 such that (A−λI)x=0(A - \lambda I)x = 0(A−λI)x=0.
    • dim(N(A−λI))>0\text{dim}(\mathcal{N}(A - \lambda I)) > 0dim(N(A−λI))>0.

      • => N(A−λI)≠{0}\mathcal{N}(A - \lambda I) \ne \{0\}N(A−λI)={0}

    • For any scalar α\alphaα, A(αx)=λ(αx)A(\alpha x) = \lambda (\alpha x)A(αx)=λ(αx) also holds.
    • If Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx and Ay=λyAy = \lambda yAy=λy, then A(x+y)=Ax+Ay=λx+λy=λ(x+y)A(x + y) = Ax + Ay = \lambda x + \lambda y = \lambda (x + y)A(x+y)=Ax+Ay=λx+λy=λ(x+y)

Simple cases

  • The eigenvalue of the zero matrix is the scalar λ=0\lambda = 0λ=0. All nonzero vectors are eigenvectors.
  • The eigenvalue of the identity matrix is the scalar λ=1\lambda = 1λ=1. All nonzero vectors are eigenvectors.
  • The eigenvalues of a diagonal matrix are its elements on the diagonal. The unit basis vectors are eigenvectors.
  • The eigenvalues of a triangular matrix are its elements on the diagonal.
    • Because if there is a zero on the diagonal, it is singular.
  • The eigenvalues of a 2 × 2 matrix can be found by finding the roots of p2(λ)=det(A−λI)=0p_2(\lambda) = \text{det}(A - \lambda I) = 0p2​(λ)=det(A−λI)=0

Defective matrices

  • A defective matrix is a square matrix that does not have a complete basis of eigenvectors, and is therefore not diagonalizable. In particular, an n × n matrix is defective if and only if it does not have n linearly independent eigenvectors.

Relative Definitions

Eigenspaces

  • the nullspace A−IλA - I\lambdaA−Iλ is the eigenspace of A for λ denoted by EA(λ)\mathcal{E}_A(\lambda)EA​(λ). In other words, EA(λ)\mathcal{E}_A(\lambda)EA​(λ) consists of all the eigenvectors of A for λ and the zero vector.

Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity

  • Example: Let A=[1210]A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}A=[11​20​]
    • -1 is an eigenvalue of A. and the correspond eigenvector is [−11]\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}[−11​]
  • EA(−1)=Span([−11])\mathcal{E}_A(-1) = \text{Span}(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix})EA​(−1)=Span([−11​])
  • The geometric multiplicity of an eigenvalue λ of A is the dimension of EA(λ)\mathcal{E}_A(\lambda)EA​(λ)
    • the geometric multiplicity of −1 is 1.
  • The algebraic multiplicity of an eigenvalue λ of A is the number of times λ appears as a root of pAp_ApA​.
    • −1 appears only once as a root. the algebraic multiplicity of -1 is 1.
  • In general, the algebraic multiplicity and geometric multiplicity of an eigenvalue can differ. However, the geometric multiplicity can never exceed the algebraic multiplicity.
  • If for every eigenvalue of A, the geometric multiplicity equals the algebraic multiplicity, then A is said to be diagonalizable.

Singular Matrix

  • A matrix is singular if and only if 0 is one of its eigenvalues. A singular matrix can be either diagonalizable or not diagonalizable. For example:
    • (1000)\left(\begin{array}{c c} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{array}\right)(10​00​) is diagonalizable
    • (0100)\left(\begin{array}{c c} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{array}\right)(00​10​) is not diagonalizable.
